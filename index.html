Coronavirus Mental Health

Trends of mental health needs in 2020 and 20201

Data as of October 2021

The first year of the COVID-19 pandemic meant an extended quarantine, a situation the majority of the current population of the United States has never had to deal with. This new way of life brought many challenges, some seen and others more subtle. One of these more abstract challenged was mental health needs. Many were faced with deteriorating mental health either for the first time or more severely than normal due to the isolation and helplessness prevalent during 2020. Some turned to medicines or counseling, but others were unable to access such resources.

From August of 2020 through mid-October of 2021, the U.S. Census Bureau received survey data from families across the country as part of the Household Pulse Survey. These internet surveys were meant to track the impacts of the pandemic on each household, and they included data such as employment status, spending, food security, housing, educational disruptions, and mental health status. The data was broken down by responders’ age, race, gender, and sexuality, as well as other dividing factors. Survey answers were submitted after every period of four weeks, allowing collectors to track the data over time as the status of the pandemic changed along with people’s views of the virus.


Below, find graphs measuring the percentage of needed versus recieved counseling or therapy during the three phases of September in 2020 and 2021. In this case, needed counseling applies to help that was needed but not recieved.

Sep 2020

According to the graph of 2020, the percentages of respondents who needed therapy or counseling but did not receive it and those who received therapy or counseling were comparable throughout September, though the first category was always slightly higher.

2021

The 2021 data shows that these percentages were still comparable, though they were both a couple percentage points higher than in 2020. Though the full lockdown was during 2020, mental health concerns seem to be higher during the second year of the pandemic.


The graphs below measure the percentage of needed versus recieved counseling or therapy during the first phase of September in 2020 (top) and 2021 (bottom). The data is divided by age groups along the x-axis

Needs by Age

When broken down by age, the data collected for the Household Pulse Survey makes it clear that young people have struggled the most with issues involving mental health during the pandemic. When compared over the two years, the percentages were generally higher in 2021 as shown in the above graphs. One differentiating factor with this data is the fact that percentages for those who were able to receive help are higher than percentages for those who didn’t among the older population. In 2020, this group included those over 50, while in 2021 this included those over 60.

Takeaways

Between the data shown above, it becomes clear that mental health concerns have only become more important as the pandemic has continued, especially for young people. Though many may have assumed mental health needs to be worse during the full quarantine in 2020, this data supports the opposite claim. Beyond this, when the data is broken down by age it is clear that resources for mental health needs have become harder to access in the last year, even as need has risen. Whether this is due to an unwillingness to accept help or simply an inability to find help remains to be seen, but it is clear mental health concerns should remain in the forefront of any pandemic action plans, now and in the future.
